What is Blockchain?

By Akhil Karra. November 27th, 2018.

Blockchain is the technology behind cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin. The Economist calls it "the next big thing." But, what is it?

Simply put, a blockchain of a cryptocurrency is a book of records that tracks which person has what amount of cryptocurrency. This ledger is open to the public and records how much cryptocurrency each person has ("The next Big Thing"), and each transaction that has occured. Owning cryptocurrency simply means that one record on the blockchain is alloted for you with the amount of cryptocurrency you have (Kharpal). The identities of those who own cryptocurrencies are sophistically encrypted, however, everything else is open to the public. In fact, anyone can download a copy of a blockchain. Blockchains cannot be tampered because of the underlying process that makes it work (click here to learn more).

But what are cryptocurrencies? They are forms of currencies that only exist online and use blockchains at their source ("Cryptocurrency"). A currency is a system of tokens used to exchange goods or services. Cryptocurrencies differ from regular currencies since they operate on a deflationary model instead of an inflationary model. In regular currencies, as the economy grows, more tokens (such as coins and notes) are produced, making something cost more over time while retaining the same value. This is called inflation. Cryptocurrencies have a limited supply, so something costs less over time while retaining the same value. This is called deflation. Three famous examples of cryptocurrencies are Bitcoin, Etherium, and Ripple (Tillier). 

Blockchains are most famous for being used in cryptocurrencies, however, there are other useful applications for them. For example, blockchain's robust nature makes it ideal to prevent voter fraud. Using blockchain technology during elections protects the voter's identity, make sure that votes cannot be tampered with, and give a real-time accurate running tally since the voting numbers would be completely public. A startup called "Follow My Vote" has already started doing this (click here for their main website). Another example would be the use of blockchain technology in hospitals. Hospitals can ensure that their patients' electronic medical records are secure, confidential, and yet easy to share data with the use of blockchain technology, thanks to its public yet impenetratible nature. Although Blockchain as a technology is still in an exploration phase, these solutions to worldwide problems seem very promising ("What is Blockchain Used For Besides Bitcoin?").
